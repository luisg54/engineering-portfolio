Overview

Concurrently with BIW work, I was assigned to lead the engineering side of the Optimus Academy — Tesla's initiative to transform a legacy Model S/X production line into a dedicated training facility for Optimus humanoid robots.

The scope covered everything from floor plan development and station layout to HRC (Human-Robot Collaboration) safety cell specification and coordination with the architect for permitting sign-off.

Facility Design & Floor Plan
  • Developed Phase 1 AutoCAD floor plan from legacy DWG files and on-site measurements — the existing drawings were incomplete and required field verification
  • Iterated through 4 floor plan revisions coordinating with architects; v4 sent for permitting sign-off
  • Laid out 40 training stations within the existing facility footprint, optimizing for robot task variety and pedestrian flow
  • Mapped and preserved all existing 480V electrical drops, pneumatic lines, and PLC cabinet locations to avoid costly infrastructure changes
HRC Safety Cell Design

Human-Robot Collaboration cells require a specific safety architecture — any gap in the specification means the safety logic either fails to protect or trips too conservatively and kills productivity.

  • Specified 36" maintenance clearance zones and 48" pedestrian clearance zones per ISO/TS 15066 HRC guidelines
  • Specified 80/20 extrusion cell framing, light curtains, and SICK/Keyence safety scanners for each station
  • Preserved existing Allen-Bradley GuardLogix PLC cabinets — avoiding complete safety logic reprogramming by mapping new sensor inputs to existing cabinet I/O
  • Coordinated sensor placement with sight-line analysis to eliminate blind zones within each cell
Lessons Learned
Legacy infrastructure is a constraint, not a problem to solve
The instinct is to rip out the old electrical and start clean. The reality is that preserving existing 480V drops and PLC cabinets saved weeks of permitting and tens of thousands in electrical work. Working around constraints creatively is a real engineering skill.
Safety specs have to be right before anything else gets built
Getting the HRC clearance zones and scanner placement wrong means the whole cell either fails safety audit or shuts down constantly from false trips. I learned to read the safety standards directly rather than assume someone else had already interpreted them correctly.
Facility design is a coordination problem as much as a technical one
The floor plan went through 4 revisions not because the engineering changed, but because architect requirements, permitting feedback, and operational constraints kept evolving. Managing that iteration loop without losing track of the design intent was the real challenge.
